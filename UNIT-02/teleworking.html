Case Study - Teleworking

Patrick O'Hara - CP1D

Teleworking is where an individual who works for a company, works at home or away from the traditional office for a certain period during the working week. This usually includes the use of electronic means of communications, like computer and Internet access, to communicate with the company.

An information intensive job is one that involves the almost exclusive processing or inputting of data or information.

Suitable jobs for teleworking are accountants, programmers and travelling salespersons.
Unsuitable jobs for teleworking are factory workers, shop assistants and those who work in service industries like chefs.

Vast improvements in telephone networks, computer technology, mobile phone networks and Internet communications have all made teleworking possible. Also employers’ acceptance of the security and speed of new technologies to allow people to work at home has made teleworking possible.

A telecentre / telecottage is a remote centre that allows an individual access to communications equipment like phones and computers so that they are able to communicate with their office without having to commute too far.

For the mother: She will be able to stay at home with her children or be able to wait for them after school. This will save her from getting a babysitter. She wouldn’t have to commute as much, which would save money on car costs. It would also save her from looking for another job.

For XYZ Ltd: They would be able to keep a highly skilled employee and they wouldn’t have to look for someone to replace the mother. They may also get an improved workload from the mother because of the relaxing atmosphere at home.

  1. Employee and job selection: The employer has to ensure that the employee chosen for teleworking is trustworthy and will be able to do their job quickly and efficiently.
  2. Employee terms and conditions: The employer must be sure of how much the employee is expected to work at home and they must be ensured that the employee will stick to those guidelines.
  3. Management style: The employer must be confidant that the employee will be able to work to deadlines and to the best of their abilities without the need for constant managerial supervision.
  4. Health and safety: The employer must make sure that the correct health and safety guidelines are implemented in the home and that the workplace must be kept as accident free as possible.
  5. Technical requirements: The correct technical requirements must be installed in the employees home e.g. phone lines, fax machines and computer access, so that easy communication between the employer and the remote employee is possible.

The teleworker themselves could feel detached from the rest of their colleagues. They may not have the same chances to socialise with others than if they worked in the office all the time. They may also feel that they have a reduced status within the company because they are not in the office all the time. As a result they may feel that they have a lesser chance of promotion than their colleagues.

What I understand by the phrase “Virtual Organisation” is an organisation that has no physical presence in the world. They would have no headquarters, no office blocks just a network of remote employees spread out across a country or the world.

I don’t believe that a “Virtual Organisation” is a realistic way in which organisations will operate. While it may be technically possible I don’t believe that the public would fully trust an organisation that they cannot actually see or visit for themselves.
